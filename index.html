Toothpick on Their New EP, Their Future, and Video Games

Interview Alternative Metal Nu Metal Industrial Rock Dallas Texas

Toothpick is a relatively new-ish (if we're going off release dates) alternative metal/nu metal band from the land of Dallas, Texas. They publicly released their first single less than a year ago, and went on to release their first EP, Change is Everything, one month ago on March 20th, 2026. While the amount of content they've put out there is fairly limited, it already feels like they've begun making a proper name for themselves. After listening to Change is Everything, it was really easy for me to see - or hear - just how authentic and differentiated they are. From the lyrics, to the vocals, tone, mixing, to basically everything, this EP definitely scratched an itch in my brain for a breed of music I didn't really even know that I had. Of course I could write about it, but I figured, "Why not just see if I can actually talking to them about it?" Luckily enough for me, they are some really cool people who were kind enough to lend me some of their time. And after talking to them, I must say that I am extremely excited to how this band evolves and where their music takes them. Without further ado:

Hi there, this is Brandon interviewing Dallas band Toothpick. If y'all could give us your names and roles in the band, please?
Jack: I'm Jack. I play drums.
Chaz: My name's Chaz, I play bass.
Collin: My name is Collin. I play guitar.
Cleo: I'm Cleo. I play guitar, I sing, and I program.

How have y'all been going? Your EP, Change is Everything, just came out a few weeks ago, so how does it feel to finally have that project out for everyone in the world to hear?
Cleo: It's like a fucking huge weight off my shoulders, dude.
Collin: Great hearing all the great feedback we've been having.
Chaz: I'm glad it's our and we can move on.

Do you guys have, like, a specific favorite song from the EP?
Cleo: That's like choosing our favorite kid, man. We sat with this EP for, like, a year, so it's kind of hard, but I will say my favorite song to play live is Suppressor. It's just super buttery smooth.
Jack: I think my favorite to play live is Ashdown.
Collin: Undoubtedly mine is Ashdown. It's a beautiful song.
Chaz: I think my favorite track off the EP is Ashdown, but if I had to pick my favorite one of those songs to play live, probably Pulgasari.

I think that one might be my top track from the EP. But it seems like Ashdown is the favorite kid.
Cleo: Yeah, Ashdown is crazy. I wrote the song and I fully expected people, like when the EP came out, to be like, "Ok there's three awesome nu metal songs, and then Ashdown" right? But it seems like from all the response we've got it seems like Ashdown is the song that people are, like, connected with the most. Outside of Aluminum.

Ok, cool. I know you said it's like a weight off your shoulders since you've been sitting on it for, like, a year now, but what was the journey like putting it together? Like, from writing the songs, finalizing the songs, recording them, and just getting the whole thing released finally?
Chaz: Well Cleo wrote the songs, and I mean the recording was mainly Cleo and I in the studio. The two of us and our producer, Evan Bostelmann. It certainly went in its own direction when we were in the studio. Not that it's a bad thing, it's just it took itself on differently.
Cleo: Yeah, we didn't expect...we learned a lot while we went into the studio about, like, we came in with this mentality of, like, how it's gonna sound in our heads. And getting to where we wanted it was a lot more of a tedious process than we thought it was going to be. So, it came out different, but I think it honestly came out with a better product than what we were expecting. It was a long, arduous march to the end, but I think it came out with a much better product than what I expected.

And Jack, when did you join the project, because if I remember correctly I read y'all's interview in What's Nu? Magazine that the Bleed and Narrow Head drummer…did he record the drums for the EP?
Jack: Yeah, so he did that before I joined, and all the drums were recorded and done before I really joined and it was just a journey of being, like, "All right, now I gotta learn how to do this shit," you know? And you get to a point where we're gonna be able to play it live, but it's a lot of fun for me because I took balance learning between what Carson played and also what Cleo had originally programmed, so what I play live is usually a bit different than it is on the album, so I really enjoy that, personally.

Cleo, I also read in that interview that the origins of Toothpick kind of stem from when you first wrote Aluminum while working with Bleed on their album, and I'm just kind of interested in knowing more about, like, how you got to doing that with the band and how that experience pushed for the formation of Toothpick.
Cleo: Well, when Toothpick started, Aluminum was really the first Toothpick song that was written. And I remember I was working with the Bleed guys on the song that would turn into Through the Cylinder, and everybody knows that record had taken, like, fucking five years to produce. I feel like every interview I talk about Bleed. But that record took like five years to produce, and in between that I wrote a couple different parts for that record - for Through the Cylinder - and one time I was just like, "These guys aren't fucking gonna do anything at this rate, I might as well write my own song" so I wrote my own song. So I wrote, like, basically what I jokingly refer to as like a parody Bleed song. Not to downplay Aluminum, because Aluminum is also, like, a very, very important song for me in my musical history. But, yeah, I was kind of, like, trying to do something like that and I put the break at the start, and that was originally supposed to be Through the Cylinder. That's where that came from.

Yeah, I remember that long Bleed wait very well.
Cleo: I remember it extremely well. I remember being frustrated. I was like "What are you guys doing?"

So when did Chaz and Collin come in?
Cleo: Me and Chaz started the band. Me and Chaz started the band from, like, the ruins of an indie rock band that we had. We formed the band and we got Carson to do the EP…it's funny, Jack was originally supposed to be on the EP. He was gonna be the first session drummer.
Chaz: Oh yeah, I remember that. I forgot about that.
Cleo: Yeah, he was gonna be the session drummer on there but there was a schedule conflict. I ended up drinking with Carson one night and I mentioned that, and Carson looked at me and he was like, "Bro I'll do it," and I was like, "...WHAT. You're fucking with me. You're fucking with me dude, seriously?" Then he worked on it, and it was just, like, so shocking that when I went back around to Jack and I was like, "Hey, you wanna play for Toothpick?" he was like "Yeah, of course." And Collin came pretty much immediately after that. Then the lineup really solidified at that point. What was the first show we played? I think you guys joined, then we played our first show…
Collin: I think it was at The Nines.
Cleo: Yea that was our first show at home base at The Nines. We practiced for like 2 months.
Collin: It was pretty short. It went pretty quickly.

By the time you had your first show did you already have the four songs off the EP done?
Cleo: Oh yea, definitely. We had them all written. Most of our set, including stuff that we haven't released, has all been written and demoed out. It's just like going to the studio, getting the money to go to the studio, working out all the stuff is just, like, a long, arduous process. It's like, songs that we wrote, like, 2 years ago won't come out until 2 years after we wrote them. So yeah, this is all to say there's plenty of Toothpick material, we're just waiting to put it out.

That's good that you mentioned, like, unreleased projects, because I also know that you said that a little while ago you would've called yourself a more "straightforward rock band" but that you've kind of been…I don't want to say experimenting but that you've been experimenting with synths and other effects like that. And I think you can hear, like, that differentiation on Ashdown if I'm not mistaken, compared to the rest of the EP. So what do you think that kind of branching out is gonna sound like for your music as you go on?
Cleo: A lot of synths, a lot of 90s…I had a conversation with a couple people about, like, playing synths in a rock band and you end up sounding like a goth band, and we're trying very hard to have electronics and have synthesizers in our band without sounding like we're going out on stage in leather and latex. I feel like most of our repertoire electronic wise is, like, 90s breakbeat, a lot of digital hardcore…old 90s hardcore, not the new stuff. We don't want to sound like Femtanyl. Sorry.
Jack: I'm going full guns blazing.
Cleo: Random Femtanyl diss, random Femtanyl mention. I love Femtanyl, Femtanyl's cool. It's just we all have a very specific taste in electronic music where it's, like, a lot of that stuff like trance synths, supersize acid synths don't get touched on a lot by rock musicians, so it's like let's do stuff people normally don't do as rock guys.

So was Ashdown the first foray into that with the EP?
Cleo: Oh yeah, absolutely. Everyone remembers hearing the demo, it sounds like a fracking Nuclear Daisies song. It sounds, like, really baggy, really 90s Madchester, almost like Primal Scream and Chapterhouse and stuff like that. Stuff like that rock guys really aren't trying to emulate nowadays.

So what electronic groups do you guys listen to?
Cleo: Let's go around. Run around the board.
Chaz: Electronic that I've been listening to lately, at least in that same vein…
Cleo: I'll start with the electronic stuff, I guess. My repertoire is a lot of, obviously, I love the old digital hardcore recordings like Christoph de Babalon, Atari Teenage Riot, Bomb20, that type of thing. Like the really gritty, aggressive stuff, but I also love The Prodigy, ET is really great, just like some more trance techno type stuff. I really like Surgeon. Surgeon's really cool. Very, like, Splinter Cell music. If I listen to electronic music it's gotta be, like, super in your face aggressive or, like, super big and, you know, textural almost. But it's usually 90s stuff. You guys?
Chaz: I think I'm ready. A few big ones off the bat that at least have influenced me a lot: Cold Cave is up there. I know we're just talking about shitting on goth stuff. I'm not a big fan of the guy anymore but I like Cold Cave. Gary Numan, shout out Gary Numan. Chemlab, Chemlab's a big one. And I'm gonna say - this is a terrible pick - Thom Yorke's solo electronic bullshit that he does.

Jack, do you have any electronic inspo?
Jack: I think I probably listen to the least electronic music out of anyone here.
Cleo: You listen to a lot of Food House and stuff. Hyperpop stuff.
Jack: Oh yeah, so it's probably shit like that, and then I like more, like, modern stuff where it almost kind of leans into hyperpop shit. Like Food House like Cleo was mentioning, I really like them a lot. It's just mostly random bullshit that's really fast and hyper, you know?
Chaz: Collin?
Collin: Well I have a little curated because I've been thinking on this. One of the newer bands that I like a lot electronic-wise is Kill Alters. Kill Alters is great. Some older 90s stuff, kind of more 2000s: LCD Soundsystem's great. Kinda leaning into the more nu metal side a little bit, The Mad Capsule Markets. And then kind of more industrial - it's definitely more industrial - I am a big fan of Bowery Electric. Another one I enjoy a lot is Model/Actriz.
Chaz: Tuco put me on Bowery Electric. Shoutout Tuco.
Cleo: Shoutout Tuco.

Obviously, DFW - that entire area - has an incredibly rich and talented scene with a ton of amazing bands. So how did your experiences - we can include or exclude Bleed from this - with that surrounding scene influence you both personally and musically?
Jack: I think that growing up, one thing that's always been really prevalent in Dallas is the shoegaze scene, you know? Like, there's always been a lot of shoegaze and that shit coming out for the past 20-30 years here. And so I think when you're just in it, you're around it, you start to kinda just see that and you get influenced by it. There's also, like, a huge metalcore and hardcore scene here, too. So just being around and seeing those bands, you kind of get a sense of like, alright, this is what's really working, you know, like this is what sounds good and it's fun to play. Another point is you see all these amazing bands and they're really fucking good so your like, alright that's where the bar is, right? Just because there's so many people here means that there's such a dichotomy, you know? And so we always want to be in that upper echelon of, like, performance and shit and that's always challenging here. Which is good.
Cleo: We try to keep at arm's length from listening too much and reading too much into, like, whatever is happening in the sounds of other local bands because if we start listening to other local bands all the time, I feel like we start to - especially me as a songwriter - get self conscious. I'm like, "Oh, I should write songs like that, I should write songs like this band," and I start getting distracted from the process of, like, let's write the best possible song that we can write and not ride on somebody else's coattails.
Chaz: I gotta say, growing up in the music scene in Dallas, it used to be terrible. Like, the early 2010s there was nothing. It was all a bunch of nothing that went nowhere. I mean, you had your imports from Houston and Austin. A lot of great music coming out of Austin, not really Dallas until I would say the late 2010s, 2020. I think that's when it really started picking up. That's also when a lot of new people were moving to Dallas. I don't know, Dallas kind of sucked for, like, the early 2010s. Nothing really going on and as a kid I never went to local shows. There were no local shows. You had to go up to Denton, which is north of Dallas, it's a college town. There was nothing going on in Dallas, and now since 2020 at least or the late 2010s, there's actually shit going on and shit worth listening to.
Collin: Especially in places like Fort Worth with like Trauma Ray.
Chaz: Yeah, Fort Worth's got a lot going on right now.
Cleo: Fort Worth's firing on all cylinders and I think Toothpick's goal is to fire on all cylinders in Dallas and tip the scales a little.
Chaz: That's my opinion.

It obviously takes a lot of guts to just write and even perform music. As musicians and artists who make music to be heard by other people, do you ever find yourselves dealing with, like, anxiety about what you're writing or dealing with the feeling that something isn't good enough?
Cleo: Oh, 100%. Every song I write, every single song I write I feel like it's probably gonna suck ass. Just to be completely frank with you, I would go through revision after revision. I think that's honestly what makes Toothpick such a great project, we do really agonize over every single section of a song. From intro, verse, chorus, verse, chorus, you know, bridge, end of song - we will literally take a song that's already completed, strip it for parts, literally every single piece, like taking a guitar part, taking a chorus line, take so on and so forth and stitch new songs back together from a bunch of different parts just to make the best possible thing. And it's partially anxiety and it's partially a drive to be like, hey, we wanna make something that stands out and exists in a greater context, you know, as like these guys are firing on all cylinders here.

The rest of you can jump in, but how do you guys deal with those feelings?
Collin: Ignore it.
Chaz: I've learned in my recent years to know when to walk away from something and when to come back. I think every creation, every song, no matter how shitty it is, has one great idea in it. Sometimes you just gotta walk away and come back to it a couple months later and you see that idea, you see what made it great and you take that and kind of recycle it. That's what I've learned recently.
Collin: It was definitely a learning process on how to deal with it and handle it.
Cleo: Especially since we've gotten in the phase of all collectively working on songs together. The way that it goes is we will just come up with tiny little parts and iterate on those parts, then it's usually my job to stitch them all together. If somebody in the band is like, "I don't like how that's stitched together," we'll strip it back, rip it to pieces, and sew it back together to make, like, new versions. Just iterating and iterating, really trying to make the best possible product we can generate.

Well I must say that the EP is very good. To prepare for the interview I've been listening to it all week.
Cleo: Thank you.

I forgot to ask, are there any bands that you guys wanna shout out?
Collin: Heavytrip, Mutt are undoubtedly two. Tealsky. Who else did we play with?
Cleo: Spurred.
Collin: Spurred's great, they're also out of Fort Worth. I know they're buds with Trauma Ray, they're great.
Cleo: Mass Appeal is great. Scourge is fantastic. Cynical. Obviously Bleed and Narrow Head. Ritual is badass. Nuclear Daisies of course, they're the goats. You guys got any? Jack, Chaz?
Chaz: I mean you said it all. There's this band from Dallas called True Widow. They're not around anymore, but their members just released a new single in a new band today called Bad Stuff. That's the name of the band, and I listened to the single and it's pretty good.
Jack: I'm trying to think of who I can say that y'all haven't already said. Shoutout Somebody Else, I really fuck with those guys.
Cleo: Shouting out guys I'm in a band with. Gene Ross is really good, and Somebody Else. They all share members and Jack is in Gene Ross, they're all a really good cohort.

Thank you for sharing your picks. I just have a couple more questions and they're gonna be slightly more fun. So, individually, what are y'alls favorite video games, or top 3 or something?
Chaz: I can go first. Right now I'm also currently playing through it again, I'm a huge Pokémon fan and I'm playing through HeartGold and SoulSilver again right now to 100% the Pokédex and I am soft resetting on two copies trying to get Shiny Ho-Oh. But the Pokémon games in general, if I had to pick my favorite Pokémon game it'd either be Platinum or Crystal, and if you don't like those answers then I'm going with Fallout: New Vegas.
Cleo: I'll go next. I'm a Source engine aficionado. I love Source engine games, so my favorite game of all time is Team Fortress 2. I think it's just a brilliantly designed game. One of my other favorite games is the original F.E.A.R. for the PC and the XBox version. Super cinematic, fantastic game. I've also been playing a lot of Helldivers 2, and that's just to say that's the one that's on my mind a lot recently. Also, I had one more: Mirror's Edge for the XBox 360. I take a lot of inspiration for Toothpick aesthetics from that game.
Collin: I would say undoubtedly, just because I grew up with them, probably the Souls games, and then definitely Valve games. I've played a lot of Half-Life on my dad's computer. Games I'm playing right now, I don't really play games as much anymore, but I'm playing Deadlock. Deadlock's not bad, it's alright.
Chaz: It's fun. I'm fucking terrible at it.
Collin: Yea it's pretty fun. But there's such a big learning curve to it, but I've also played a lot of Tarkov, I'm very much into competitive games. I have poured countless hours into those that I will not get back.
Which was that one?
Collin: Escape from Tarkov, it's an extraction shooter.
Chaz: It's a very bullshit game where if you hate yourself, if you wanna ruin your life, play that game.
Jack: I fucking hate that game, it's all bullshit. I'm probably gonna go with Mordhau as my first, because that's the game I put the most hours in. The best way to describe it is like CoD, except you're knights and shit. It has a really unique and hard to learn combat system, so I've spent so many hours playing that shit. Then probably the first Kingdom Come. Love that game. I need to think of another one that isn't knight shit that I like. You know what, I'm probably gonna go with Fable III. Growing up…oh my God I love that game.
Chaz: Shoutout Peter Molyneux.

Ok, thank you guys for sharing those answers. This is really my last question for you guys. What is the best live show that you have ever been an audience member in?
Chaz: I know my pick. 2023, I went to Oklahoma City to see Viagra Boys open for Queens of the Stone Age. I'm a huge Viagra Boys fan, huge Queens fan, and I got front row, I got to meet Sebastian Murphy after the show. He's a great dude, chiller. He's the vocalist for Viagra Boys. I was front row, and they had an opener, Jehnny Beth. She's a French artist, I wanna say. She was trying to get me to hold her up on the guardrails but I couldn't hear her because I'm hard of hearing and I had earplugs in and she was just looking at me with the mic in her hand and I was like, "I can't hear you." The guy next to me had to hold her up and it was a great show. It was awesome. Viagra Boys kills it every time.
Jack: I don't know if I can choose a best show, but I think one of my most memorable was seeing Amon Amarth, Lamb of God, and Slayer on like their second final tour. I think this was like 2016-2017, so I was really young, I was like 13 or 14, and just being in a pit for Slayer, you know, it was raining and muddy as fuck, so it was just everyone there in this massive pit, so that was something where I was like, "Ok, this shit is actually really cool, I like this," you know? So it's all been either uphill or downhill from there depending on how you view it.
Cleo: This is actually a fairly recent one. I got to see Nine Inch Nails for the first time. Nine Inch Nails is my favorite band of all time. Kind of has informed my entire musical career, and I got to see them play all the hits live and it was fantastic. I work in live music, so I have a super big appreciation for production. The amount of work that those guys have put in behind the scenes to make everything, the light show was incredible, the whole stage production, everything going on was fantastic. They played all the hits. Trent - still at sixty-something years old - still sounds amazing, and it was, like, truly life affirming to see that band life. It was amazing. Nine Inch Nails: 2026 Peel it Back Tour. Was it the Peel it Back Tour? I don't know, whatever tour they just played was fantastic, amazing show.
Collin: A memorable one for me was either in 2024 or maybe 2025. It was Poison The Well and Glassjaw. That was pretty great. Especially Poison The Well I love Poison The Well, so to actually see them play was pretty cool. So yea, definitely one of my tops.
Cleo: Glassjaw was badass. That probably would've been my second or third if not for Nine Inch Nails.
Collin: Also, I have to say it, but Death Grips. The mosh pit there was awful. You would have shirtless dudes fall on you, and you'd get these sweat imprints. But getting to see Zach Hill, MC Ride and them, it was kind of surreal, almost. There's such an internet culture with them and I've only ever seen them online forever, so being able to actually see them, that was pretty surreal.

Well those were all the questions I had for you guys. Is there anything else that you guys want to share or mention?
Cleo: You fuck with Chevelle?
Me? Yeah.
Cleo: You're gonna like what we got coming up next.
Dude, really?
Cleo: You're gonna like our next studio project.
Ok awesome. Are there any other details you can share? Is it like a full length album?
Cleo: I wanna keep it vague, I wanna keep it vague.
Ok, well that sounds exciting.

heat death of the universe — Dead Butterflies

Review Screamo Skramz Electronic Emo Fort Worth Texas

"(Scattered on your arms!) / I saw the twinkle in your eyes fade / (Stay on the phone!) / We held hands in the summer twilight / (Scattered on your arms!) / We hold a blanket of blades" — checkered arms

Second post let's go. I had many ideas for what my second post should be about, but this album slipped through my headphones and into my ears at the last second and I knew that I had to talk about it.

heat death of the universe is the third full length album from Fort Worth skramz and screamo band Dead Butterflies. I think that this band can also be labeled something like "electronic screamo" due to the awesome electronic elements incorporated into most of their songs. Whenever I try to talk about Dead Butterflies or this album I always try to explain it like "if a Sonic the Hedgehog OST had a baby with a screamo band." I also saw a Bandcamp comment that said it had "Undertale skramz vibes" so I think my analysis checks out. I'm not really well versed in the technical aspects of electronic music production — or even music production in general — to really go into more depth on that front. They're also the only skramz band I know whipping out trumpets during their shows, but maybe I'm also not well versed in the skramz trumpet scene.

Speaking of Dead Butterflies live shows, I got the chance to see them for the first time in Houston opening for Catalyst and sharing the stage with Salvinorin-A, Empty Shell Casing, Blister, and Lagrimas. These bands are all extremely worthy of their own posts but let's stay on topic. Anyways, Dead Butterflies' set had to be one of the most beautiful sets I've seen in my life. Usually these shows are full of people going crazy, bouncing off the walls, and stagediving on top of you, and this set was no different. However, there was just something different about it. This one just seemed to radiate bliss and pure fun and infected everyone in the venue with it. The band was in great spirits moving around and having fun with the crowd and the crowd was super engaged and stagediving every which way. Meanwhile, the people toward the back were just dancing blissfully to the super energetic electronic skramz being played. It was really the most joyful set I've been an audience member in. It's kind of hard to describe it, but it's true.

All of that was an extremely long winded way to start talking about the actual album, which is the point of this whole post. Like I said, this is the third Dead Butterflies album, preceded by Ramona, The Hurt and I See Myself In You, I Hate It. I very nearly wrote this post about Ramona, The Hurt which nearly made me cry but I won't keep getting off topic. But if Ramona, The Hurt's electronic elements were at around a 5 or 6, then heat death of the universe cranks that up to around an 8 or 9. It meshes so well with the other instruments to completely amplify the vibe and effect each song has on you.

Speaking of which, I can't think of an album in recent memory that just completely throws you back and forth on the emotional spectrum like this one does. There are songs like "starfall on our lonely planet", "exploding heart syndrome", and "midnight" that are just unfettered electric energy that will make you bounce off the walls, while songs like "heat death of the universe", "checkered arms", and "in my bones" are much more sorrowful and sincere, their emotional effect amplified by the massive enveloping soundscapes. Literally just imagine the Family Guy bit where Peter is crying in his car to one song then immediately happy and dancing to the next. But seriously, the more solemn songs — along with the rest of the album, of course — are so insanely beautiful and massive, with "in my bones" being the explosive emotional finale to the entire album.

This album succeeds in bringing together so many things in a phenomenal way. It is fun, energetic, emotional, sad, and made by a band who seems like they are genuinely trying to make beautiful, expressive art. If you want to go crazy and dance or if you want to lay in a fetal position, this album has it all. I am very proud of Texas music. Beautiful beautiful band and album.

  • 2. butterfly eyes
  • 3. beneath the surface
  • 4. midnight
  • 5. heat death of the universe
  • 6. starfall on our lonely planet
  • 7. exploding heart syndrome
  • 8. checkered arms
  • 9. in my bones

Keep the Flow — Folder

Review Nu Metal Rap Metal Italy

"I can't feel with all my obsession / 'Cause I can't do it with all my perseveration / But I can do it, and do it again / 'Cause I'm sexy but not the sexiest" — Cut the Belt

Ok so I've decided that my very first blog post ever will be about the 2001 album Keep the Flow by Folder. I realize that it's kind of a strange pick to kick off the blog since I wanted to try to keep the blog focused on cool newer artists, but I've just been listening to this album a lot lately. Therefore, I want to write about it.

First of all, I feel like it should be stated that this band is indeed Italian. Despite listening to this album for a while I had literally no knowledge of this until I decided to do a little bit of research for this blog post. As a Texan, I can't really recall any other time I've heard about an Italian nu metal band. In hindsight though that does kind of make sense and would explain the unique pronunciation of some of the lyrics — it was an Italian accent all along.

I'm not really exactly sure how well known this band is, but I think that they're pretty popular in the nu metal community. Personally, I've only ever seen other people talk about them a couple of times in obscurity on the internet. Without thinking too hard about the other classic nu metal albums out there — like any early Limp Bizkit album — I would say that this is one of the wackiest I've heard in the best way possible. In other words, this album is one of the most fun that you can find within the nu metal genre.

The riffs are incredibly fun and are at times very effects heavy while having the kind of energetic properties that make any listener want to bounce off the walls, whereas most nu metal would make you want to break stuff (EBK: elite ball knowledge). This combined with the DJing and record scratches transform the album into pure energy. Now, I'm pretty sure the lyrics were written in Italian and then translated to English, so they may sound off to some and may not entirely be grammatically correct. However, I think this only adds to the album's strengths. The pronunciation and vocalist's overall sound only help give the vocals a unique sound that stands out from other bands. This element specifically is what I think gives the album its wacky nature and only makes it more entertaining to listen to.

As for the actual tracks, there are a staggering 14 of them. My personal top tracks at the moment are "Left", "Closer", and "Divine Stitches." I personally think "Left" is the perfect representation of how wacky this album can get in the best way. However, none of these are my actual favorite. That spot would be reserved for the classic "Cut the Belt." This song is incredibly awesome and does everything right, building up right at the beginning into a drop of loud drums, record scratches, vocal chops, and a hard, bouncy riff. From there, all instruments drop out except for the low vocals and scratchy guitar, only to build up again with a really funky pre-chorus that primes you for the chaotic chorus. Repeat everything, and that's basically "Cut the Belt", the highlight of this album (at least in my opinion).

I wouldn't really say this album is for everyone, and perhaps not even for every nu metal fan as it is a tad unconventional. But for me, this album is one that I think should help represent the rest of the genre — at least in some ways — because I just think that it really encapsulates how goofy, fun, and entertaining the genre can be. It basically just sounds like some punks just got in a studio and had some great fun with it. I hope hypothetically that if anyone in the band read this they would take all that as a compliment, because who knows if that's what they were going for.
